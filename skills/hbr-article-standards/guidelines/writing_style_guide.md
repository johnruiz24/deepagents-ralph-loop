# HBR Writing Style Guide

## Tone: The Persuasive Expert

The HBR tone is not dryly academic; it is that of a confident, experienced expert making a persuasive case to a peer. It should be:

- **Authoritative, not arrogant.**
- **Clear and direct, not simplistic.**
- **Evidence-backed, not just opinion.**
- **Forward-looking and actionable.**

**Key principle:** Tell a story. Frame the analysis within a narrative that has a clear problem, a turning point (the insight), and a resolution (the strategic imperative).

## Language

- **Use strong, active verbs.** (e.g., "This strategy *drives* growth" vs. "Growth is driven by this strategy.")
- **Avoid jargon.** If you must use a technical term, explain it simply.
- **Be specific and use concrete examples.** Instead of "improves efficiency," say "reduces manual processing time by 40%."

## Structure of the Argument

1.  **The Hook:** Start with a surprising statistic, a provocative question, or a short, compelling anecdote.
2.  **The Problem:** Clearly define the business challenge and why it matters now.
3.  **The Counterintuitive Insight:** This is the core of the article. Present your main argument, which should challenge conventional wisdom.
4.  **The Evidence:** Back up your insight with data, case studies, and logical reasoning.
5.  **The Strategic Implications:** Explain the "so what." What does this insight mean for leaders and companies?
6.  **The Actionable Recommendations:** Provide a clear, concise set of actions that leaders can take. This is non-negotiable for HBR.
7.  **The Conclusion:** End with a powerful, forward-looking statement that reinforces the urgency and importance of your message.
