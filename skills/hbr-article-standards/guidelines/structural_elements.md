> # HBR Structural Elements Guide

An HBR article is not just a block of text. It is a carefully structured document designed for maximum readability and impact for a busy executive audience. The following structural elements are **mandatory**.

## 1. Author Byline and Credentials

- **What:** Identifies the author and establishes their credibility.
- **Where:** Immediately after the article title and subtitle.
- **Example:**
  ```markdown
  by **John A. Doe**
  *John A. Doe is a professor of business administration at Harvard Business School and the author of "The Book on This Topic."*
  ```

## 2. "Idea in Brief" Sidebar

- **What:** A 100-150 word summary of the entire article, broken into three parts.
- **Where:** Typically on the first or second page, in a visually distinct sidebar.
- **Structure:**
  - **The Problem:** What is the business challenge or question?
  - **The Argument:** What is the author's core, counterintuitive insight?
  - **The Solution:** What are the key actions or takeaways for leaders?

## 3. Pull Quotes

- **What:** Short, impactful sentences or phrases pulled from the main text and displayed in a larger font.
- **Where:** Placed strategically throughout the article to break up long text blocks and emphasize key points.
- **Quantity:** 2-3 per article.
- **Example:**
  > "The biggest risk isn't failing to adopt AI—it's over-automating the human experiences that customers value most."

## 4. Inline Visuals with Captions

- **What:** All charts, diagrams, and images must be placed directly within the text where they are discussed.
- **Positioning:** A figure should appear immediately after the paragraph that first references it (e.g., "...as shown in Figure 1.").
- **Captions:** Every visual MUST have a numbered caption (e.g., "Figure 1: Market Growth Trajectory").

## 5. Subheadings

- **What:** Use clear, often provocative subheadings to break the article into logical sections.
- **Purpose:** Guide the reader through the argument and allow for easy scanning.
- **Example:** Instead of "Analysis," use "The Double-Edged Sword of Automation."
